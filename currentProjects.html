Here are some of my current research projects. If you are interested in collaborating or learning more, please do feel free to reach out.

There are also a number of student projects that are currently available for Masters Projects, so if you are interested in supervision, please let me know.

Understanding within-host dynamics of mycobacterium tuberculosis infection

This project develops a mathematical model to describe the within-host dynamics of Mycobacterium tuberculosis (Mtb) infection. This project aims to gain insights into the progression of the disease and the host's immune response. We focus on the early stages of infection, to attempt to identify key factors that may influence the outcome of the infection.

Modelling T-cell response and dendritic cell interactions

This project focuses on developing a mathematical model to describe the interactions between T-cells and dendritic cells during an immune response. We develop an agent-based model to simulate the dynamics of these interactions, and derived a continuum PDE model to provide a mathematical tractable framework for understanding these complex processes.

Tumour-immune dynamics and tumour cell resistance

This project investigates the complex interactions between tumours and the immune system, focusing on how tumours evade immune detection and develop resistance to the immune response. By understanding how tumours undergo selective pressures to become resistant to the immune response, we aim to identify key factors that influence tumour progression.

Autonomous electric vehicle discharge to the grid

As electric vehicles become more widespread, they provide a unique opportunity for grid support through vehicle-to-grid. One challenge is to ensure that the discharge of energy from these vehicles is optimised to meet grid demand, in an organised manner. This project will aim to develop a mathematical model to describe the discharge of energy from electric vehicles to the grid, and to identify risks for managing this process.

Understanding Malaria and Dengue prevalence in low incidence settings

This project aims to develop a mathematical model to understand the dynamics of malaria and dengue transmission in low incidence settings. By performing inference on real incidence data, we seek to identify intervention strategies to reduce the burden of these diseases.
